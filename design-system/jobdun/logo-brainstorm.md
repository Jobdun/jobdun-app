## Brand DNA (the thesis the geometry must argue for)

- **Audience**: Australian tradies — chippies (carpenters), sparkies (electricians), plumbers, brickies, concreters, builders, site supervisors, crew leads.
- **Voice**: Blue-collar, direct, no-fluff, gets-it-done. The opposite of fintech-bright SaaS branding.
- **The name**: *"Job"* + *"Dun"* — phonetic "done." Lines up with Aussie slang **"done and dusted"** and **"job's right."**
- **Visual posture**: Aggressive flat. Dark slate `#0F172A`, safety orange `#F97316` (the colour of every hi-vis vest, every traffic cone, every excavator). Heavy weight. No gradients, no soft shadows, no fintech polish.
- **Mission of the logo**: Sit comfortably on a hard hat sticker, a ute door magnet, a high-vis vest patch, a worksite signboard — and *not* look out of place next to a Bunnings green or a Caterpillar yellow.

## The 6 famous-logo schools we drew from

Each logo concept below has a "school" — an iconic logo we're learning a move from. Use these as **search anchors** when you Google for patterns in Australia.

| School | Iconic example | The move |

# 🥇 Concept 1 — DUN STAMP / "Get it Dun" ⭐ LEAD

**Files**: `lib/core/assets/logo-concepts/dun-stamp-j/{mark.svg, lockup.svg}`

```
[ ][ ][ ][ ]
      [ ]
      [ ]
[ ][ ][ ] [█]   ← orange stamp escapes the J at bottom-right
```

### Story
The orange block **escapes the J's grid** — it's not part of the letterform. It lands beside the J like a stamp slammed onto a finished work order. The brand name becomes a verb. The geometry *argues*: every job here travels from posted → matched → **DUN**. The "Dun" of the name is rendered in the negative space outside the J.

### Why it leads
- **The name fuses with the mark.** Like Apple (bite), FedEx (hidden arrow), or Amazon (a→z smile), the logo *is* the wordplay. This is the strongest brand-name-to-logo move available.
- **It owns a verb.** *"Get it Dun"* is a tagline you can print on hoodies, hard hats, ute decals, app store screenshots, Bunnings-aisle posters. Cornerstone/Keystone are *moments*; DUN is the *outcome* — and tradies pay for outcomes, not process.
- **It speaks the audience's slang back to them.** Aussie tradies say *"done and dusted"*, *"all sorted"*, *"job's right"*. The logo speaks blue-collar English, not LinkedIn English.

### Australian Market Search Patterns
Use these search terms to find visual/cultural parallels:

- `"Australian Made" stamp logo`
- `"approved stamp" construction Australia`
- `Master Builders Australia approval mark`
- `HIA (Housing Industry Association) certification logo`
- `BSA (Building Services Authority) tick / stamp`
- `Australian Standards mark` (the white tick in red circle)
- `WorkCover Australia logo`
- `tradesman certified stamp Australia`
- `"job done" tradie meme Australia`
- `Aussie completion stamps QA`

### Cultural anchors to validate against
- **Australian Standards "tick"** — the most trusted approval mark in AU. If our stamp can sit alongside it without looking foreign, we win.
- **Master Builders / HIA logos** — peer-association marks tradies recognize.
- **Bunnings green tick** — the "we've got it" reassurance.
- **Toyota Hilux ute decals** — what tradies actually stick on their truck doors.

### Brand line options
- *"Get it Dun."* (strongest)
- *"Job's Dun."*
- *"Dun and dusted."*
- *"Stamp it Dun."*

### Risks
- "Dun" is unusual spelling — some users may misread as a typo on first contact. **Mitigation**: the visual stamp pun lands instantly on second look.

# 🥈 Concept 2 — TILTED STAMP

**Files**: `logo-concepts/dun-stamp-tilted/`

```
[ ][ ][ ][ ]
      [ ]
      [ ]
[ ][ ][ ] [▣]   ← orange stamp rotated 10°, slightly oversized
```

### Story
Same DUN STAMP DNA, but the orange square is **rotated 10°** and slightly larger — like a real rubber stamp slammed by a tradie's hand on the back of a job sheet. Off-axis = real, hand-made, lived-in. Not a digital icon.

### Why it works
The tilt converts the stamp from "rendered in software" to "applied by a human." That tiny rotation says: *a person finished this work and stamped it themselves.*

### School / parent logos
**Levi's red tab** (the most famous tilted-stamp ever applied to clothing), **USPS rubber stamps**, **vintage council-approved stamps**, **retro tradie certification stickers**.

### Australian Market Search Patterns
- `Levi's red tab logo`
- `vintage rubber stamp Australia`
- `Australian council approved stamp retro`
- `old tradie certification mark`
- `vintage Bunnings stamp` / `1950s Aussie hardware brand`
- `"PASSED" rubber stamp Aussie inspection`

### Cultural anchors
- **Council certificate stamps** on Aussie building permits — usually off-axis when applied by hand.
- **Retro Australian Made stamps** (pre-digital era).
- **Vintage Akubra hat tags / RM Williams boot stamps** — the tilt-applied authenticity feel.

### Brand line
- *"Slammed by hand."*
- *"Stamped real."*

### Risks
- Rotation can look "designed" if too perfect — needs to feel *spontaneous*, not *decorative*.

# 🥉 Concept 3 — VERIFIED STAMP

**Files**: `logo-concepts/dun-stamp-verified/`

```
[ ][ ][ ][ ]
      [ ]
      [ ]
[ ][ ][ ] [✓]   ← orange stamp contains a white check
```

### Story
The stamp now contains a white ✓ inside it. Double-encodes completion: the stamp *says* "Dun," and the check inside *seals* it. Reads as **officially authorized**, app-store-approved, government-stamped work.

### Why it works
Combines DUN STAMP's wordplay with the universally understood ✓ symbol — the most legible "done" signal in any language. Even before reading "JOBDUN," a user sees the ✓ and gets the brand promise.

### School / parent logos
**Twitter verified badge** (blue ✓), **App Store / Play Store approval marks**, **Australian Standards tick**, **government approval seals**.

### Australian Market Search Patterns
- `Australian Standards tick logo` (red circle with white ✓)
- `Australian Made green-and-gold tick`
- `MBA Master Builders tick logo`
- `verified tradie badge Australia`
- `"licensed" tradie stamp Australia`
- `government approved mark Australia`

### Cultural anchors
- **Australian Made campaign logo** (green triangle + gold kangaroo) — the gold standard for AU approval marks.
- **Australian Standards tick** — instantly read as "this passes the law."
- **Service NSW / Service Victoria badges** — official government tick aesthetic.

### Brand line
- *"Officially Dun."*
- *"Tradie verified."*
- *"Stamped & sealed."*

### Risks
- Adding the ✓ inside the stamp can make it visually busy at small sizes (favicon, hard-hat sticker). Worth a 16×16-pixel test.

```
      [ ]
[ ][ ][ ] (◎)   ← orange concentric seal
```

### Story
The stamp is now a **concentric circle** — orange disc with an inner ring and centre dot. Reads as a notary seal, embossed document mark, or premium-product certification. Carries the most *authority* of any variant — heritage-grade, official, unimpeachable.

### Why it works
A circular seal beside square brick letters creates intentional contrast (round vs. grid) that the eye notices. The seal element brings gravitas — the kind that lets the brand feel old-world trustworthy.

### School / parent logos
**Notary public seals** (worldwide), **presidential seal**, **wax seals on letters**, **JP (Justice of the Peace) stamps**, **premium-product certifications** (e.g. Champagne AOC).

- `Australian heritage trust seal`
- `premium product seal Australia`
- `Master Builders Association badge round`
- `tradesman guild seal Australia`
- `Australian Made gold seal`

### Cultural anchors
- **JP stamps** — every Aussie knows what a JP stamp looks like; ours can carry that same official weight.
- **Australian War Memorial seals** — heritage authority aesthetic.
- **National Trust of Australia** — premium-heritage feel.

# 🏅 Concept 5 — ARROW STAMP

**Files**: `logo-concepts/dun-stamp-arrow/`

```
[ ][ ][ ][ ]
      [ ]
      [ ]
[ ][ ][ ] [▶]   ← orange right-pointing arrow
```

### Story
The stamp is a **right-pointing arrow** — the job leaves the platform, moves forward, ships to the next stage. Reframes "Dun" not as a full stop but as **forward motion**. Each completed job kicks the next one off.

### Why it works
This is the most growth-oriented variant. Where every other stamp says "this is finished," the arrow says "this is shipped — what's next?" — language that appeals to the *next-job* mindset, not the *last-job* mindset.

### School / parent logos
**FedEx hidden arrow** (the world's most famous logo Easter egg), **Amazon a→z arrow**, **DHL forward chevrons**, **Auspost** (the Australian equivalent).

### Australian Market Search Patterns
- `Australia Post logo evolution` (red chevron progression)
- `StarTrack courier logo Australia`
- `Toll Group logistics Australia`
- `Aussie shipping arrow logo`
- `"shipped" stamp Australia`
- `forwarding mark logistics`
- `Auspost parcel stamp`

### Cultural anchors
- **Australia Post** — the universal "delivered / shipped" feeling in AU.
- **StarTrack, Toll, Linfox** — Aussie freight & logistics aesthetic.
- **VicRoads / Main Roads ute decals** — forward-arrow + work in progress.

### Brand line
- *"Shipped."*
- *"Next job's ready."*
- *"Job moves forward."*

### Risks
- Arrow can read as "delivery / logistics company" rather than "trade matching platform." Distinct from the brand category — pro or con depending on positioning.

### Story
The stamp is an **8-point starburst** — a comic-book impact mark. Visualizes the moment of completion as a **shockwave**, not a quiet checkbox. The most aggressive, high-energy variant — built to feel like a punch landing.

### Why it works
For an audience that swings hammers and runs drills, "impact" is the native vocabulary. The burst converts the abstract idea of "job done" into a physical-feeling moment.

### School / parent logos
**Mr. Clean impact burst**, **comic POW! / BANG! / WHAM!**, **action-sports impact stars**, **AFL & NRL impact marketing graphics**.

### Australian Market Search Patterns
- `AFL impact graphic` / `NRL impact star`
- `Aussie sports logo burst`
- `Cricket Australia impact mark`
- `Tradie tools impact promo Australia` (e.g. Bunnings, Total Tools)
- `"BOOM" / "BANG" comic impact star`
- `1950s Australian advertising burst`

### Cultural anchors
- **AFL goal celebrations** — visual energy Aussies viscerally recognize.
- **Big W / Kmart sale-blast graphics** — retail burst aesthetic.
- **Total Tools / Sydney Tools promo graphics** — burst stars on hardware sales.

### Brand line
- *"Hit. Done."*
- *"BOOM. Dun."*

### Risks

## 🇦🇺 Australian-Market Cultural Lens (cross-concept)

Use these patterns to test ANY of the concepts against the Aussie tradie audience:

### Colour benchmarks
- **Safety orange** `#F97316` — matches AS/NZS 4602 hi-vis day-only orange. ✅
- Sits comfortably next to: Bunnings green, Caterpillar yellow, Toyota Hilux red, RM Williams brown.
- Doesn't compete with: emergency red (`#EF4444`), Coles red, BHP green.

### Typography benchmarks
- Bold condensed sans matches: Aussie roadwork signs, council notices, hardware store price tags, Bunnings aisle markers.
- Avoid: thin serifs (too white-collar), script (too feminine for this audience), tech-startup geometric (too Silicon Valley).

### Iconography to AVOID
- Suit-and-tie figures, briefcases, dollar signs, charts/graphs — all read "office," not "site."
- Crisp drop-shadows, gradients, glossy buttons — all read "consumer SaaS" rather than "industrial tool."

### Iconography that LANDS
- Stamps, badges, tools, hard hats, hi-vis stripes, tape measures, spirit levels, hex bolts, scaffold geometry, brick patterns, weld marks.

### Slang the brand can use
- *"Dun and dusted"*
- *"Job's right"*
- *"Sorted"*
- *"Knocked over"* (= completed)
- *"Smashed it"*
- *"All sweet"*

### Slang to AVOID (US-coded)
- *"Crushed it"* (too US-startup)
- *"Killing it"* (off-vibe in trades)
- *"Boss" / "Hustle"* (US influencer language)

## How to use this doc

1. **Open it next to the in-app `/logo-compare` page** (button at bottom of login). Compare each concept's visual against the story here.
2. **Run the search patterns** — Google or Pinterest the terms under each concept. Save 2-3 reference images per concept. Which feels most "Aussie" without trying too hard?
3. **Show it to a tradie.** Literally one chippie or sparky in your network. Ask: *"Which of these looks like it belongs on a hard hat sticker?"* That answer cuts through 90% of design debate.
4. **Pick a winner.** Tell Claude the concept name and the winning mark + lockup overwrites `lib/core/assets/{logo-jobdun.svg, mark-jobdun.svg}` — all 8 reference sites in the app update automatically.
